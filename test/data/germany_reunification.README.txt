West German reunification dataset
=================================

File: germany_reunification.csv  (long format: country, year, gdp, + predictors)

Source
------
Abadie, A., Diamond, A., & Hainmueller, J. (2015). "Comparative Politics and the
Synthetic Control Method." American Journal of Political Science, 59(2), 495-510.

The canonical West German reunification panel used throughout the synthetic-
control and interactive-fixed-effects literature -- the standard second benchmark
after Proposition 99, and the demonstration dataset for Xu's `gsynth`
interactive-fixed-effects package. The vendored CSV is a faithful export whose
GDP values match the published panel exactly (West Germany per-capita GDP: 2284
in 1960, 20465 in 1990, 28855 in 2003).

What is vendored
----------------
17 countries observed annually 1960-2003 (44 years each, balanced panel):

    country   country name; treated unit = "West Germany"
    year      1960-2003 (integer)
    gdp       per-capita GDP, PPP-adjusted USD (the outcome `y`)
    code, infrate, trade, schooling, invest60, invest70, invest80, industry
              the original Abadie predictors / identifiers, kept for provenance;
              the GeoCausality estimators use only `gdp`

West Germany reunified in 1990, so it is treated from 1990 onward. The other 16
OECD countries form the donor pool.

Published result this fixture reproduces
----------------------------------------
Abadie, Diamond & Hainmueller (2015) estimate that reunification lowered West
German per-capita GDP by roughly $1,600 per year on average relative to the
synthetic control, with the gap widening over time. Our `SyntheticControl`
recovers ~-1,644 (average post-period gap), and the interactive-fixed-effects
estimators (`GeneralizedSyntheticControl`, `InteractiveFixedEffects`) land in the
-1,800 to -2,000 range -- same sign, magnitude and significance.

Note: Abadie et al. match on several covariates (trade openness, schooling,
investment, inflation) in addition to lagged GDP, while the GeoCausality
estimators use only the GDP trajectory; the headline effect is reproduced
nonetheless. The published figure is a "right ballpark" benchmark rather than a
single precise ATT, so the parity tolerance is deliberately generous.
